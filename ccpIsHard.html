The Challenge of Measuring Canopy Cover with LiDAR

A critical look at why top-down methods can misrepresent complex ecosystems like savannas.

A Common Method: Hysteresis Thresholding

This two-stage method defines tree canopy from a 2.5D Canopy Height Model (CHM). It first identifies "core" canopy pixels above a high threshold (e.g., 2m), then expands to include any adjacent, lower pixels that are physically connected. This logic has profound consequences in mixed ecosystems.

Pixels with height > 2.0m are marked as "definitive crown areas."

Stage 2: Hysteresis Expansion

Adjacent pixels with height > 0m are absorbed into the crown if they touch the core area.

Issue 1: The Mechanism of Overestimation

The method's goal is to prevent "artificial fragmentation" of a single tree crown. However, in savannas, this creates **artificial aggregation**. The algorithm's reliance on 2.5D horizontal proximity causes it to misinterpret a separate understory shrub as a low-hanging part of the tree, merging distinct ecological strata and inflating the final canopy measurement.[1]

Issue 2: The Flaw of 2D Representation

Creating a Canopy Height Model (CHM) from a top-down Digital Surface Model (DSM) discards the majority of the 3D data. This view cannot distinguish a dense tree from a sparse one with shrubs underneath, a crucial distinction in savannas.[1, 2]

The method correctly identifies the true canopy boundary.

Scenario B: Realistic Savanna Condition

The method incorrectly includes adjacent shrubs, overestimating canopy cover.

A Simpler, Validated Approach for CPC

For the specific metric of Crown Projective Cover (CPC), research in analogous Australian woodland ecosystems identified a much simpler, more robust method. This approach avoids complex delineation algorithms and their associated errors entirely.[3]

Conclusions & Recommendations

Complex delineation methods are often unsuitable for savanna ecosystems. For accurate results, analysis must move beyond 2.5D models and embrace the full 3D nature of the LiDAR data.

  • ✓

    Direct 3D Point Cloud Classification

    Use machine learning to classify each point in 3D space (e.g., 'high canopy', 'shrub', 'ground') *before* calculating cover. This preserves vertical separation and prevents erroneous merging of strata.

  • ✓

    Voxel-Based Stratification

    Convert the point cloud into a 3D grid of 'voxels'. This allows for the calculation of canopy cover for specific height layers (strata) independently, providing a true multi-layered view of the ecosystem.

  • ✓

    Multi-Sensor Data Fusion

    Combine airborne LiDAR's structural data with the spectral information from hyperspectral sensors. This allows for classification based on both plant height and species type, offering the most ecologically robust delineation.
