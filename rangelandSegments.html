Integrating advanced geospatial analysis with Indigenous ecological knowledge to create a dynamic, multi-scalar classification framework for Northern Australia.

Foundation Unit

The Challenge: Beyond Static Maps

Traditional land classification systems often fail to capture the dynamic and variable nature of Australia's vast rangelands. They are frequently static, too coarse for local management, and lack the ability to integrate different knowledge systems.

This new framework proposes a paradigm shift: a bottom-up, data-driven approach that builds a rich, hierarchical understanding of the landscape from the ground up, while respectfully weaving in the deep ecological knowledge of Indigenous Australians.

A Six-Level Hierarchical Taxonomy

The framework organizes the landscape into a nested hierarchy, scaling from a single hectare to entire bioregions. Each level represents a distinct scale of ecological pattern and process, providing a comprehensive view for planning and management.

LEVEL 6 Bioregion

A very large area with common climate, geology, and characteristic biota.

Scale: >1,000,000s ha | Example: The 'Top End Coastal' Bioregion

LEVEL 5 Sub-Bioregion

A large area of broad geomorphic and macro-climatic similarity.

Scale: 100,000s - 1,000,000s ha | Example: The 'Daly Basin' Sub-Bioregion

LEVEL 4 Land System

A recurring pattern of ecofacets linked by common geology and hydrology.

Scale: 1,000s - 100,000s ha | Example: The 'Wave Hill Land System'

LEVEL 3 Ecofacet

A recurring mosaic of associated ecotopes defined by a specific landform.

Scale: 100s - 1,000s ha | Example: A 'Lateritic breakaway'

LEVEL 2 Ecotope

The smallest ecologically distinct, relatively homogeneous landscape unit.

Scale: 1 - 100s ha | Example: 'Ribbon grass on a cracking clay flat'

LEVEL 1 EcoSegment

The foundational base unit; a nominal 1ha grid cell or polygon.

Scale: 1 ha | Example: A single 100x100m data pixel

A Legacy of Landscape Classification

Our proposed framework builds upon a rich history of ecological classification. Understanding these foundational systems highlights the specific gaps our data-driven, multi-scalar approach is designed to fill.

IBRA Framework

The national standard (Interim Biogeographic Regionalisation for Australia) that classifies the continent into 89 Bioregions and 419 Subregions.

Strength: Provides the essential macro-scale context for conservation planning.

Limitation: Too coarse for property-level management or dynamic condition monitoring.

CSIRO Land Systems

Pioneering work in Australia that defined a Land System as a recurring pattern of geology, topography, soils, and vegetation, identified from aerial photos.

Strength: Highly relevant to the patterned nature of Australian rangelands.

Limitation: Primarily qualitative/descriptive; not easily derived from modern digital data.

Queensland Regional Ecosystems

A state-wide system that classifies and maps vegetation communities based on their geology, landform, and characteristic flora.

Strength: Provides a detailed, legally mandated framework for biodiversity conservation.

Limitation: Primarily focused on remnant vegetation and expert-defined classes.

NT Rangeland Monitoring

A system focused on assessing pastoral land condition, building on the Land System legacy with a network of ground monitoring sites (e.g., Tier 2 sites).

Strength: Directly tailored to pastoral management with strong field-based assessment.

Limitation: Site-based data can be difficult to extrapolate across vast, heterogeneous areas.

Two Ways of Knowing: Weaving Knowledge Systems

This framework's core innovation is bridging these two powerful worldviews. The hierarchical taxonomy provides a common, structured language to describe the patterns of Country, while the connectivity analysis models the processes that link them, creating a unified view that respects and integrates both scientific and Indigenous perspectives.

Western Science: Landscape Ecology

Landscape ecology uses quantitative methods to model the landscape as a mosaic of patches and corridors. Graph theory helps analyze this structure, identifying critical habitats and pathways for species movement and resource flow.

Indigenous Knowledge: Songlines

Songlines are a profound, process-oriented framework of connectivity. They represent pathways of Ancestral Beings, encoding deep ecological knowledge about resources, navigation, and the reciprocal relationship of "Caring for Country".
