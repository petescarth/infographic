SeRGS

Sequential Linear Regression Slopes

Core Question:

"Is the ecosystem's fundamental ability to convert rain into biomass getting weaker or stronger?"

What it Measures:

The trend in the slope of the Vegetation-Rainfall Relationship. It's a measure of functional efficiency.

Best for Detecting:

Gradual, long-term "press" disturbances like sustained grazing pressure or the effects of rising temperatures.

Key Strength: ‚úÖ

Statistically robust even during severe, ongoing degradation. Its moving window approach maintains analytical validity.

Key Weakness: ‚ùå

Parameter selection is a trade-off between spatial and temporal precision. Can blur the exact timing of events.

TSS-RESTREND

Time Series Segmentation & Residual Trend

Core Question:

"Has an abrupt event occurred, and what are the productivity trends before and after?"

What it Measures:

The trend in residuals (unexplained productivity), but it first segments the time series at abrupt breakpoints.

Best for Detecting:

Abrupt, event-driven "pulse" disturbances like fire, land clearing, or severe drought.

Key Strength: ‚úÖ

Excels at dating and quantifying the impact of major events, providing powerful forensic analysis capability.

Key Weakness: ‚ùå

Can be sensitive to climatic extremes, potentially misinterpreting a severe drought as a structural break in the system.

The Future is Fractional

The single biggest improvement for rangeland monitoring is moving beyond the ambiguous NDVI signal. The future lies in applying these powerful methods to disaggregated, fractional cover data. By analyzing trends specifically in perennial grass, shrub, and tree cover, we can finally get a clear, managerially relevant picture of land condition change.
