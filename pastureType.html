Smart Pastures

Mapping Australia's Grasslands for Producer Success

The Producer's Dilemma

Australia's grazing industry relies on vast and variable pasturelands. For producers, matching feed availability with livestock demand is a constant challenge, impacting profitability and sustainability. This project aims to bridge the information gap by creating the first wall-to-wall map of grass functional types for the entire continent.

The View From Above: Data & Technology

High-Resolution Satellite Data

We use time-series satellite data captured every 5 days, from 2016 to the present. This provides an incredibly detailed look at how pastures change across seasons and years.

Each pixel of the map is broken down into three key components: Green Fraction (active growth), Dry Fraction (cured vegetation), and Bare Ground. This "fractional cover" product gives a precise measure of ground conditions.

Advanced Analysis: From Pixels to Pasture Phenology

We analyze the entire time-series for each pixel to extract its unique "phenological signature"â€”the fingerprint of its annual growth cycle. This allows us to classify the underlying vegetation type with high accuracy by identifying key differences in their growth patterns.

Annual vs. Perennial Grasses

Perennials maintain a base level of green cover year-round, while annuals have a distinct, sharp peak followed by senescence.

Tropical (C4) vs. Temperate (C3) Grasses

These types have asynchronous growth seasons. Temperates (C3) peak in cooler, wetter months, while Tropicals (C4) thrive in summer heat.

Developing a Robust Classification Model

Testing Classification Methods

Multiple machine learning algorithms will be tested to find the most robust and accurate classifier based on the extracted phenology metrics.

Beyond Certainty: Probabilistic Outputs

Instead of a single "best guess," our final model will produce a probability for each of the four grass types. This provides a measure of confidence and is vital for advanced risk analysis, showing which areas are mixed or transitional.

Proposed End-to-End Workflow

1. Data Foundation

Utilize the 5-day time series of Cibo Labs Sentinel 2 satellite-derived fractional cover (green, dry, bare) from 2016 to the present as the primary input, with rainfall, temperature, and elevation as ancillary data.

↓

2. Pre-processing

Test the HANTS algorithm applicability to seasonal and episodic time series data across Australia, and apply this or a similar algorithm to each pixel's time series to smooth noise, reject outliers, and create a complete, gap-free dataset representing the true phenology.

↓

3. Feature Engineering

Transform the time-series data for each pixel into a rich feature vector by calculating a comprehensive suite of phenological decompositions, metrics, harmonic analysis parameters, and multi-year statistical measures that capture the distinct temporal signatures of the four target grass classes. Augment this with static ancillary data (climate, topography, soils).

↓

4. Ground-Truthing

Assemble a continental-scale, harmonized training and validation dataset by systematically acquiring and processing data from the Atlas of Living Australia (ALA), the Terrestrial Ecosystem Research Network (TERN),state-level vegetation survey databases (e.g., QLD's QBEIS, NSW's BioNet) and Producer volunteered data, such as farmer observations and paddock-level information made available as part of CRC projects.

↓

5. Classification

Test an ML classifier, trained on the ground-truth data, to predict the dominant grass functional type (Temperate Annual, Temperate Perennial, Tropical Annual, Tropical Perennial) for every 30-meter pixel across Australia.

↓

6. Application

Link the final classification probability map to actionable feed quality parameters (Digestibility and Metabolisable Energy) through a dynamic model that uses the real-time green-to-dry fraction ratio from the satellite data to adjust baseline values according to the pasture's phenological stage.

Phase 1: The NSW Northern Tablelands Pilot Study

To ensure our national map is accurate, we will begin with a focused pilot study in the Northern Tablelands of New South Wales. This region is ideal due to its diverse mix of temperate and subtropical grasses, and both native and improved pastures.

Pilot Objectives

  • Test and compare machine learning models (Random Forest, XGBoost, LSTMs).
  • Calibrate models using intensive local ground-truth data from NSW DPI and LLS.
  • Fine-tune phenological metrics for distinguishing local grass species.
  • Develop a robust accuracy assessment protocol before national scaling.

From Map to Menu: Fueling Feed Calculators

The ultimate goal is to provide critical inputs for feed budgeting tools like ProGraze and EverGraze. The type of grass has a direct and significant impact on its feed quality for livestock.

Temperate (C3) grasses are generally more digestible and energy-dense than Tropical (C4) grasses at a similar growth stage, which fundamentally changes calculations for stocking rates and animal performance.

The Four Key Grass Functional Types

The final map classifies every pasture pixel in Australia into one of four functional types. This powerful new dataset will empower producers to more accurately manage their feed base, optimise stocking rates, and build more resilient and profitable grazing enterprises.

Temperate Annual

These C3 grasses germinate in autumn, grow through winter, and set seed in spring. They provide high-quality winter feed but create a summer feed gap. E.g., Annual Ryegrass, Silver Grass.

Temperate Perennial

The backbone of southern grazing systems. These C3 grasses persist for years, providing a reliable feed base with peak growth in spring and autumn. E.g., Phalaris, Perennial Ryegrass, Cocksfoot.

Tropical Annual

Opportunistic C4 grasses that germinate after summer rains, grow rapidly in the heat, and are typically lower in digestibility than temperate types. E.g., Button Grass, some Sorghum varieties.

Tropical Perennial

Durable C4 grasses forming the basis of northern pastures. They are highly productive in summer but dormant and low-quality in winter. E.g., Mitchell Grass, Buffel Grass, Kikuyu.
